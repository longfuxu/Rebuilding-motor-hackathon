A two-half-stroke P→H→P cycle with a descending staircase of four unequal substeps

Dwell = ascend (P→H, ATP loading); burst = descend (H→P, Pi-release power strokes). The four workers step back to planar one at a time; because the staircase treads are uneven, the four substeps differ (avg 2.5 bp). One special subunit anchors DNA and does not translocate.

A. The cycle. A planar ring (P) loads ATP and rises into a helical staircase (H, lock-washer) during the dwell; Pi release drives it back to planar (P) during the burst, translocating ~10 bp. B. The burst as a descending staircase: the four worker subunits step down to planar in sequence, each driving DNA by its own tread height. Treads are unequal (illustrative 2.8 / 1.4 / 1.7 / 4.1 bp, from our ENM staircase, normalized to sum 10) — not four identical 2.5-bp steps. The special subunit (E) is the non-translocating anchor. C. Packaged-bp vs time for one cycle: our uneven staircase (solid) vs the textbook 4×2.5 (dashed). Falsifiable: 3D-MINFLUX per-subunit z(t) resolves equal vs graded, position-dependent substeps. Corrections folded in: the 8.3 Å ring "seam" is just the staircase span (max−min), not a physical hinge; Liu-2014's 10→8.9 bp change is a slow capsid-filling/pressure effect, separate from the within-burst inequality.
